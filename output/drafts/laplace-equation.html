Laplace Equation

Posted on May 18, 2019 in Physics

Laplace eqation is:

$$\Delta \psi = 0 $$

Some physical applications where laplace equations arrise naturally are listed below: 1. Steady state temperature distribution in the region with no source or sink 2. electrostatic potential in a region with no charge density 3. gravitational potential in a region with no mass 4. velocity potential in a region with no source or sink

Do you see some pattern here ? Of course you do.

Before talking about specific cases of these applications, let's talk about the equation itself. Let's see what kind of function satisfy the Laplace equation.

Let's first look at one dimentional example. Laplace equation in one dimension is:

$$ \frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial x^2} = 0 $$

We can solve this...

$$ \partial^2 \psi = 0 $$

Integrate twice in a row to get:

$$ \partial \psi = a $$
$$ \psi(x) = ax + b $$

which is a equation of a straight line.

Before we look at higher dimension, let me assert some properties of this soultion:

  1. the value of the function at a point is the average of the value at points at equal distance
$$ \psi(x) = \frac{1}{2} \left[ \psi (x + d) + \psi (x - d) \right] $$

where, d is some distance. And above holds for all possible d.

  1. The solution has no local maxima or minima and the global minima and maxima always lie on the end points i.e. on the boundary.

Of course, this satisfies in case of a straight line.

Now let's look at 2D case. Laplace equation in two dimension is:

$$ \frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial y^2} = 0 $$

This is no longer an ordinary differential equation. It is impossible to write a complete general solution to this second order partial differential equation.
